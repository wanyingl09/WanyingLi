max-flow min-cut theorem

presented by Wanying Li and Emily Ortiz

Theorem

The maximum value of an s-t flow is equal to the minimum capacity over all s-t cuts.

Basically: MAX FLOW = MIN CUT

This might not make sense now, but don't worry, we will get into it.

Terms

  • Network - a system of nodes/vertices connected by edges
  • Source (s) - all units flow from this Vertex
  • Sink (t) - all units flow to this vertex
  • Capacity (of an edge) - maximum amount of units that can fit/move along the edge
    • You can think about it as the maximum number of cars that can fit on a street!
  • Flow (of an edge) - how many units are actually moving along the edge
    • 0 <= flow <=capacity
  • Conservation of Flow - incoming flow = outgoing flow at each vertex

Let's start by putting the maximum number of units along this edge. Vertex labels have been added for clarity.

3 units flow into vertex a, so 3 units must flow out of it. You can put those 3 units along any of the different edges coming out of vertex a, but we will keep the units together and move them along this edge.

3 units flow into vertex c, so 3 units must flow out of it. One of the edges has a capacity of 2, so we will can only put 2 units along that edge. The other unit will go down the edge with a capacity of 4.

We don't have to worry about the 2 units that made it to the sink (t). The 1 unit that flowed into vertex d flows out this edge. Now, our network has a flow of 3, since 3 units came in from the source (s), and allt 3 units went to the sink (t).

So far, we have only filled one of the edges coming out of the source (s). Let's put the maximum number of units along the second edge.

Let's fix this. There is only one edge flowing out of vertex b, and it has a capacity of 3 units. Let's fill that edge to the max. If 3 units flow out of vertex b, then 3 units must flow into vertex b (Conservation of Flow).

Okay, so 3 units are flowing from b to d, and 1 unit is flowing from c to d. Vertex d has 4 units flowing into it, so there would be 4 units flowing out.

ðŸš¨ We have a problem again! The flow of this edge (4) can not be greater than the edge's capacity (3).

There is one edge flowing out of vertex d, and it has a capacity of 3, so let's put the maximum number of units along that edge, 3.

3 units are flowing out of vertex d, so only 3 units can flow into it. Let's leave the 1 unit flowing from vertex c to d. That means we can only have 2 units flowing from vertex b to d.

Since 2 units flow out of vertex b, only 2 units can flow into it.

It looks like our network is complete! It has a flow of 5, since a total of 5 units enter the network from the source (s), and exit the network through the sink (t).

How do we know this is the maximum flow?

We don't, at least not yet. A flow of 5 looks pretty efficient for our network, but how do we know it is the most efficient? Again, our theorem states that the maximum value of an s-t flow is equal to the minimum capacity over all s-t cuts. So, if we find a cut that is equal to 5, then we know 5 is the maximum flow of our network.

But what is a cut??

  • Cut - a partition (sometimes noted by a line/bubble) that completely separates s from t. There should be no path for units to flow from s to t.
  • Capacity (of a cut) - sum of the capacities of the edges that cross the cut. Add up the capacities of the edges that point to a vertex outside the cut.
  • Minimum Cut - a cut with the smallest capacity

Finding the minimum cut

We can use trial and error to try out different cuts and find their capacities. This cut has a capacity of 12. We got this by adding the capacities of the edges crossing the cut (3 + 9 = 12).

This cut has a capacity of 6. Even though the cut crosses 3 edges, we only add up the underlined capacities. 3 units along the s-a edge + 3 units along the b-d edge = 6 units. We don't include the 2 units along the a-b edge, since that edge points to vertex b, which is inside the cut.

Let's try one more cut. This cut crosses the c-t edge and the d-t edge. When we add up their capacities, 2 + 3 = a cut capacity of 5.

Conclusion

We filled our network with units and got a capacity of 5. We also found a cut that has a capacity of 5. Since 5 = 5, we have found the maximum-flow and minimum-cut of this network!

History

  • 1955 map of rail networks from the Soviet Union to Eastern Europe
  • US wanted to cut off routes from suppliers in Russia to Eastern Europe as efficiently as possible
  • Lester Ford and Delbert Fulkerson at the Research and Development (RAND) corporation made an algorithm which finds the optimal s-t cut

Uses of the Theorem

  • In computing it is used in network reliability, connectivity, etc.
  • In mathematics is used to do bipartite matching.
  • In other less technical areas, it can be used for image processing, airline scheduling, baseball elimination, and many more topics, each worth their own TED Talk.
